Frequently asked questions

What is a PoE security camera and do I need one?

PoE stands for Power over Ethernet. A PoE camera receives both electrical power and network data through a single Ethernet cable, which means you only need to run one cable per camera instead of two (a power cable and a data cable). PoE cameras connect to a PoE switch or NVR (network video recorder) that provides the power. They’re the gold standard for permanent, reliable outdoor installations because they’re wired for continuous 24/7 recording, don’t have battery limitations, and are harder to jam than Wi-Fi cameras. You need one if you’re building a serious multi-camera system and are willing to run cable through walls or under eaves.

What’s the difference between a wired PoE camera and a plug-in Wi-Fi camera?

A plug-in Wi-Fi camera connects to power through a standard outlet cable but still sends video over your Wi-Fi network. A PoE camera sends both power and video over a single Ethernet cable directly to a recorder or switch, bypassing Wi-Fi entirely. Plug-in Wi-Fi cameras are simpler to install and don’t require running Ethernet, but they share your Wi-Fi bandwidth and are subject to Wi-Fi reliability. PoE cameras are more reliable, have lower latency, and support higher-resolution continuous recording without straining your router, but require cable runs to each camera location. For most homeowners, plug-in Wi-Fi covers their needs; PoE is for those who want a professional-grade permanent installation.

Can I mix wired and wireless cameras in the same system?

Yes, and it’s a common practical approach. You might use a wired PoE camera for your main front door and driveway where you want 24/7 continuous recording, and battery-powered wireless cameras for secondary angles like a side yard or back patio where running cable is impractical. Most NVR systems accept both wired PoE cameras and wireless cameras through an IP channel, though you’ll want to confirm compatibility before purchasing. Mixing brands in the same NVR system requires checking that both use standard ONVIF protocols, which most reputable PoE cameras do.
